8 Discounting Future Impacts

Today’s Goals:

  1. Learn why and how we discount benefits and costs that occur in the future
  2. Practice computing net present value (NPV) in Excel
  3. Understand the origin and use of the social discount rate (SDR), and how varying assumptions about the SDR can affect NPV

8.1 Why Discount the Future?

When we conduct a CBA, we start from the assumption that benefits (and costs) are worth less in the future than they would be worth today. Why do we make this assumption?

  • Opportunity Costs: The resources we expend for our project could be earning a positive return elsewhere (say, in the stock market).
  • Human Nature: People prefer to consume benefits today and put off costs until tomorrow.6
  • Inflation: The price of goods tends to rise over time. $100 in ten years will buy less stuff than $100 today.7 We may want to take this into account for our analysis.

8.2 Future Value

The most straightforward way to motivate the idea of present value is to first consider its inverse: future value.

Exercise 1: Suppose you have $100 and you put it into an account that yields 3% interest each year. How much will that account be worth in one year?

\[ FV = PV(1+r) \]

Exercise 2: How much will the account be worth in two years? Five years? (Throughout, assume that interest is compounded annually.)

\[ FV = PV(1+r)^T \]

Exercise 3: Using Excel, compute the future value of the account over each of the next twenty years.

8.3 Present Value

Conceptually, computing present value of a future benefit is the inverse problem.

Exercise 4: Suppose someone promised to give you $100 in exactly 1 year. You know you can earn 4% each year in an investment account. How much would you be willing to accept today instead of the future benefit?

\[ PV = \frac{FV}{1+r} \]

Exercise 5: What if you had to pay someone $100 in 5 years? How much would you be willing to pay them today instead of that future payment?

\[ PV = \frac{FV}{(1+r)^T} \]

The net present value (NPV) of a project is the present value of benefits minus the present value of costs.

8.5 Discounting Over Multiple Periods

Exercise 7: Suppose a project yielded the following stream of benefits:

Year 0 (today): $300

Year 1: $200

Year 2: $300

Year 3: $400

Year 4: $200

Year 5: $1000

Using a discount rate of 3%, compute the present value of the project. How much would you be willing to pay today for that stream of benefits?

Here’s a useful figure from the Boardman book. See if you can replicate it in Excel (discount rate is 4%). A nice feature of computing NPV is that it doesn’t matter whether you do the net benefit step first or the discounting step first. You still get the same answer.

If the stream of benefits is constant (e.g. $X every year for N years), then computing the present value is simpler. We can use the annuity formula. \[ PV = \sum_{t=1}^n \frac{A}{(1+r)^t}=A\sum_{t=1}^n \frac{1}{(1+r)^t}=Aa_r^n \] We can compute the annuity factor, \(a_r^n\), in Excel with the PV formula.

Exercise 8: Compute the net present value of an annuity that costs $900 today and pays $100 in benefits every year for 10 years. Is NPV positive or negative?

An annuity that extends indefinitely is called a perpetuity. What’s a perpetuity worth? You’re being promised an infinite amount of money, so…infinity dollars?

Exercise 9: What’s the NPV of the annuity from Exercise 8 if the benefits kept accruing for 20 years? 50 years? 100 years? 250 years? 400 years? 1000 years?

In the limit, the value of a perpetuity equals: \[ PV = \frac{A}{r} \]

8.6 Comparing Projects

Always compare using the same discounting horizon! When comparing two projects that yield benefits over different time periods, normalize each NPV using its annuity factor. The equivalent annual net social benefit (EANB) is one such method. This converts each project into an annuity value, an apples-to-apples comparison.8 \[ EANB = \frac{NPV}{a_r^n} \]

Exercise 10: Project A costs $10,000 upfront and yields $2,500 in benefits every year for 10 years. Project B costs $20,000 upfront and yields $9,000 in benefits every year for 3 years. Assume a 6% discount rate. Which has a higher NPV? Which has a higher EANB? Which should you implement? Why?

8.7 Inflation

Prices tend to rise over time. Or, equivalently, the value of a dollar tends to decline over time.

Nominal dollars are unadjusted for inflation. Real dollars are adjusted for inflation, typically by dividing by a consumer price index (CPI).

Year Consumer Price Index Nominal Dollars Real 1982 Dollars (Nominal / CPI * 100)
1982 100 $1,000 $1,000
1990 134.2 $1,000 $745
2001 177.4 $1,000 $564
2010 220.5 $1,000 $454
2019 258.4 $1,000 $387

That’s how you convert between real and nominal dollars.

Remember this:

  • If you are working with nominal dollars, use a nominal discount rate
  • If you are working with real dollars, use a real discount rate

Just be consistent!

In practice, you will probably already be using real dollars when you make projections (it makes more intuitive sense to think about future benefits and costs at today’s prices rather than future inflated prices).

8.9 The Social Discount Rate

What is the “right” discount rate to use? As Table 10.1 demonstrates, the choice matters!9

In Week 2, we discussed why it is conceptually difficult to aggregate individual preferences into a “social preference”. The same thing goes for time preferences. Each individual in society has a different marginal rate of time preference (i.e. value on consumption today vs. tomorrow / patience). But for our analyses, we want a social discount rate (SDR). The Boardman book goes into considerable detail on methods to derive this quantity, so consult chapter 10 if you’d like to learn more. For our purposes, note that there are two competing methods:

  1. The Social Opportunity Cost of Capital (SOC) Method
  • The SDR should reflect the opportunity cost of raising capital through taxes and borrowing. If interest rates are high, there is a bigger opportunity cost from taking funds out of the private market to fund public projects. SDR under this method is proportional to private rates of return on investment and the cost of borrowing.
  1. The Social Time Preference (STP) Method
  • The SDR should balance society’s impatience with its preference for consumption smoothing. In periods of high economic growth, we can consume more today and still expect plenty tomorrow. In periods of low economic growth, we may need to save more today in order to consume tomorrow. SDR should therefore increase with the rate of economic growth.

Depending on the method you choose (and the country where you reside), you get slightly different estimates of SDR. Boardman recommends a time-declining SDR of 0.035 for US projects that last less than 50 years, dropping steadily thereafter for long-term intergenerational projects.10

Regardless of method, picking the right discount rate is inherently uncertain. So it is good practice to always perform a sensitivity analysis! That is our topic for next week.
